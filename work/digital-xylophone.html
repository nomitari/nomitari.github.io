Digital Xylophone

This digital xylophone is an interactive art installation that joins art, music, and programming using Arduino and various physical computing technologies.


Each key emits a tone and illuminates different lanterns to create both a visual and musical user experience.

Process


This installation was inspired by Japanese artist Yayoi Kusama, who created the exhibit shown above. We wanted to expand on the concept of colored lanterns and integrate them in a visualization of the musical output from a digital xylophone.

Our design uses the same colored lanterns from Kusama's work and adds a xylophone keyboard to allow the user to participate in and have control over the experience.

Before starting with our prototyping phase, we developed the basic framework for the final xylophone we would be making.

We started the fabrication process once we had a schematic diagram laid out for the inner circuitry of our xylophone.

Prototyping & Building


Though we started out using force sensing resistors, we decided that making our own analog switches for the keys in this xylophone was a better fit.

After building out our schematic with the keys, Neopixels, and a speaker, we used a laser cutter and MakerCase to build the xylophone structure out of MDF wood.

We painted designs onto plain lanterns following the Kusama-inspired polka-dot pattern and built a frame for the installation using chicken wire.

In our implementation of this xylophone installation, we built a small structure for the lanterns to hang from so that the project was portable. In our ideation, the lanterns hung from the ceiling for a more immersive experience.

High Fidelity Xylophone


After assembling the individual parts of this digital xylophone installation, we programmed an Arduino to link the key presses with tones and Neopixel lights. In addition to programming the traditional functionality of a xylophone, we also added two pre-set tunes that play alongside the notes' corresponding lanterns.
